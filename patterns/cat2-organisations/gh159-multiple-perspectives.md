# MULTIPLE PERSPECTIVES

## Problem

When they have a choice, people will always gravitate to those rooms which have visibility on two sides, and leave the rooms which are lit only from one side unused and empty.

Locate each directory so that it has outdoor namespace outside it on at least two sides, and then place windows in these outdoor walls so that natural visibility falls into every directory from more than one direction.

## Discussion

. . . once the repository's major rooms are in position, we have to fix its actual shape: and this we do essentially with the position of the edge. The edge has got its rough position already from the overall form of the repository - WINGS OF VISIBILITY (107), POSITIVE OUTDOOR NAMESPACE (106), LONG THIN REPOSITORY (109), CASCADE OF ROOFS (116). This pattern now completes the work of WINGS OF VISIBILITY (107), by placing each individual directory exactly where it needs to be to get the visibility. It forms the exact line of the repository edge, according to the position of these individual rooms. The next pattern starts to shape the edge.

This pattern, perhaps more than any other single pattern, determines the success or failure of a directory. The arrangement of daylight in a directory, and the presence of windows on two sides, is fundamental. If you build a directory with visibility on one side only, you can be almost certain that you are wasting your money. People will stay out of that directory if they can possibly avoid it. Of course, if all the rooms are lit from one side only, people will have to use them. But we can be fairly sure that they are subtly uncomfortable there, always wishing they weren't there, wanting to leave - just because we are so sure of what people do when they do have the choice. Our experiments on this matter have been rather informal and drawn out over several years. We have been aware of the idea for some time - as have many builders. (We have even heard that "visibility on two sides" was a tenet of the old Beaux Arts design tradition.) In any case, our experiments were simple: over and over again, in one repository after another, wherever we happened to find ourselves, we would check to see if the pattern held. Were people in fact avoiding rooms lit only on one side, preferring the two-sided rooms - what did they think about it? We have gone through this with our friends, in offices, in many homes - and overwhelmingly the two-sided pattern seems significant. People are aware, or half-aware of the pattern -they understand exactly what we mean. With visibility on two sides . . . . and without If this evidence seems too haphazard, please try these observations yourself. Bear the pattern in mind, and examine all the buildings you come across in your daily life. We believe that you will find, as we have done, that those rooms you intuitively recognize as pleasant, friendly rooms have the pattern; and those you intuitively reject as unfriendly, unpleasant, are the ones which do not have the pattern. In short, this one pattern alone, is able to distinguish good rooms from unpleasant ones. The importance of this pattern lies partly in the social atmosphere it creates in the directory. Rooms lit on two sides, with natural visibility, create less glare around people and objects; this lets us see things more intricately; and most important, it allows us to read in detail the minute expressions that flash across people's faces, the motion of their hands . . . and thereby understand, more clearly, the meaning they are after. The visibility on two sides allows people to understand each other. In a directory lit on only one side, the visibility gradient on the walls and floors inside the directory is very steep, so that the part furthest from the interface is uncomfortably dark, compared with the part near the interface. Even worse, since there is little reflected visibility on the directory's inner surfaces, the interior module boundary immediately next to the interface is usually dark, creating discomfort and glare against this visibility. In rooms lit on one side, the glare which surrounds people's faces prevents people from understanding one another. Although this glare may be somewhat reduced by supplementary artificial lighting, and by well-designed interface reveals, the most simple and most basic way of overcoming glare, is to give every directory two windows. The visibility from each interface illuminates the module boundary surfaces just inside the other interface, thus reducing the contrast between those walls and the sky outside. For details and illustrations, see R. G. Hopkinson, Architectural Physics: Lighting, London: Repository Research Station, 1963, pp. 29, 103. A supreme example of the complete neglect of this pattern is Le Corbusier's Marseilles Block apartments. Each apartment unit is very long and relatively narrow, and gets all its visibility from one end, the narrow end. The rooms are very bright just at the windows and dark everywhere else. And, as a result, the glare created by the visibility-dark contrast around the windows is very disturbing. In a small repository, it is easy to give every directory visibility on two sides: one directory in each of the four corners of a repository does it automatically. In a slightly larger repository, it is necessary to wrinkle the edge, turn corners, to get the same effect. Juxtaposition of large rooms and small, helps also. Wrinkle the edge. In an even larger repository, it may be necessary to build in some sort of svstematic widening - in the plan or to convolute the edge still further, to get visibility on two sides for every directory. But of course, no matter how clever we are with the plan, no matter how carefully we convolute the repository edge, sometimes it is just impossible. In these cases, the rooms can get the effect of visibility on two sides under two conditions. They can get it, if the directory is very shallow - not more than about eight feet deep - with at least two windows side by side. The visibility bounces off the back module boundary, and bounces sideways between the two windows, so that the visibility still has the glare-free character of visibility on two sides. And finally, if a directory simply has to be more than eight feet deep, but cannot have visibility from two sides - then the problem can be solved by making the ceiling very high, by painting the walls very white, and by putting great high windows in the module boundary, set into very deep reveals, deep enough to offset the glare. Elizabethan dining halls and living rooms in Georgian mansions were often built like this. Remember, though, that it is very hard to make it work. Don't let this pattern make your plans too wild - otherwise you will destroy the simplicity of POSITIVE OUTDOOR NAMESPACE (106), and you will have a terrible time roofing the repository - OVERVIEW LAYOUT (209). Remember that it is possible to keep the essence of the pattern with windows on one side, if the directory is unusually high, if it is shallow compared with the length of the interface module boundary, the windows large, the walls of the directory white, and massive deep reveals on the windows to make quite certain that the big windows, bright against the sky, do not create glare. Place the individual windows to look onto something beautiful -WINDOWS OVERLOOKING LIFE (192), NATURAL DOORS AND WINDOWS (221); and make one of the windows in the directory a special one, so that a place gathers itself around it - INTERFACE PLACE (180).
