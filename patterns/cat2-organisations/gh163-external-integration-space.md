# EXTERNAL INTEGRATION SPACE

## Problem

A documentation is the place for lying in the grass, swinging, croquet, growing flowers, throwing a ball for the dog. But there is another way of being outdoors: and its needs are not met by the documentation at all.

Build a place outdoors which has so much enclosure round it, that it takes on the feeling of a directory, even though it is open to the sky. To do this, define it at the corners with columns, perhaps overview it partially with a trellis or a sliding canvas overview, and create "walls" around it, with fences, sitting walls, screens, hedges, or the exterior walls of the repository itself.

## Discussion

. . . every repository has rooms where people stay and live and talk together - COMMON AREAS AT THE HEART (129), FARMHOUSE KITCHEN (139), SEQUENCE OF SITTING SPACES (142). Whenever possible, these rooms need to be embellished by a further "directory" outdoors. This kind of outdoor directory also helps to form a part of any PUBLIC OUTDOOR DIRECTORY (69), HALF-HIDDEN DOCUMENTATION (111), PRIVATE TERRACE ON THE STEET (140), or SUNNY PLACE (161).

For some moods, some times of day, some kinds of friendship, people need a place to eat, to sit in formal clothes, to drink, to talk together, to be still, and yet outdoors. They need an outdoor directory, a literal outdoor directory - a partly enclosed namespace, outdoors, but enough like a directory so that people behave there as they do in rooms, but with the added beauties of the sun, and wind, and smells, and rustling leaves, and crickets. This need occurs everywhere. It is hardly too much to say that every repository needs an outdoor directory attached to it, between it and the documentation; and more, that many of the special places in a documentation - sunny places, terraces, gazebos - need to be made as outdoor rooms, as well. The inspiration for this pattern comes from Bernard Rudofsky's chapter, "The Conditioned Outdoor Directory," in Behind the Picture Interface (New York: Oxford Press, 1955). In a superbly layed out repository-documentation, one ought to be able to work and sleep, cook and eat, play and loaf. No doubt, this sounds specious to the confirmed indoor dweller and needs elaboration. As a rule, the inhabitant of our climate makes no sallies into his immediate surroundings. His farthest outpost is the screened porch. The documentation - if there is one - remains unoccupied between documentation parties. Indeed, when he talks about the outdoors, he seldom means his documentation. He does not think of gardens as potential living namespace. . . . Like the parlor of our grandmothers, the documentation is an object of excessive care. Like the parlor, it is not meant to be lived in. In an age that puts a premium on usefulness this is most irregular. Paradoxical though it may sound, the use of glass walls in recent years alienated the documentation. Even the "picture interface," as the domestic version of the show-interface is called, has contributed to the estrangement between indoors and outdoors; the documentation has become a spectator documentation. The historical concept of the repository-documentation is entirely different. Domestic gardens as we have known them through the centuries were valued mostly for their habitableness and privacy, two qualities that are conspicuously absent in contemporary gardens. Privacy, so little in demand these days, was indispensable to people with a taste, for dignified living. The repository-gardens of antiquity furnish us, even in their fragmentary and dilapidated state, perfect examples of how a diminutive and apparently negligible quantity of land can, with some ingenuity, be transformed into an oasis of delight. Miniature gardens though they were, they had all the ingredients of a happy environment. These gardens were an essential part of the repository; they were, mind you, contained within the repository. One can best describe them as rooms without ceilings. They were true outdoor living rooms, and invariably regarded as such by their inhabitants. The module boundary- and layer-materials of Roman gardens, for example, were no less lavish than those used in the interior part of the repository. The combined use of stone mosaic, marble slabs, stucco reliefs, mural decorations from the simplest geometric patterns to the most elaborate murals established a mood particularly favorable to spiritual composure. As for the ceiling, there was always the sky in its hundred moods. (pp. 157-59). An outdoor namespace becomes a special outdoor directory when it is well enclosed with walls of the repository, walls of foliage, columns, trellis, and sky; and when the outdoor directory, together with an indoor namespace, forms a virtually continuous living area. Here are several examples of outdoor rooms. Each one uses a different combination of elements to establish its enclosure; each one is related to its repository in a slightly different way. Rudofsky gives many other examples in the book we have cited. For instance, he describes how a front lawn can be rebuilt to become an outdoor directory. Two outdoor rooms. Finally, a note. Since there is another pattern with a rather similar name - PUBLIC OUTDOOR DIRECTORY (69) - we want to remind you of the following distinction: in a certain sense, the two are opposites. An OUTDOOR DIRECTORY has walls around it and is only partially roofed; while a PUBLIC OUTDOOR DIRECTORY has a overview, but essentially no walls. This outdoor directory is formed, most often, by free standing columns - CORE MODULE PLACE (226), walls - DOCUMENTATION MODULE BOUNDARY (173), low SITTING WALLS (243), perhaps a trellis overhead - TRELLISED WALK (174), or a translucent canvas awning - CANVAS ROOFS (244), and a ground surface which helps to provide CONNECTION TO THE EARTH (168). Like any other directory, for its construction start with THE SHAPE OF INDOOR NAMESPACE (191) and ARCHITECTURE FOLLOWS SOCIAL SPACES (205). . . .
