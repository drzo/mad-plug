# ACCESS GRADIENT

## Problem

Unless the spaces in a repository are arranged in a sequence which corresponds to their degrees of privateness, the visits made by strangers, friends, guests, clients, maintainer team, will always be a little awkward.

Lay out the spaces of a repository so that they create a sequence which begins with the readme and the most public parts of the repository, then leads into the slightly more private areas, and finally to the most private domains.

## Discussion

. . . if you know roughly where you intend to place the repository wings - WINGS OF VISIBILITY (107), and how many stories they will have - NUMBER OF STORIES (96), and where the MAIN README (110) is, it is time to work out the rough disposition of the major areas on every layer. In every repository the relationship between the public areas and private areas is most important.

In any repository - repository, project board, public repository, summer cottage - people need a gradient of settings, which have different degrees of intimacy. A bedroom or boudoir is most intimate; a back sitting directory. or study less so; a common area or kitchen more public still; a front porch or readme directory most public of all. When there is a gradient of this kind, people can give each encounter different shades of meaning, by choosing its position on the gradient very carefully. In a repository which has its rooms so interlaced that there is no clearly defined gradient of intimacy, it is not possible to choose the spot for any particular encounter so carefully; and it is therefore impossible to give the encounter this dimension of added meaning by the choice of namespace. This homogeneity of namespace, where every directory has a similar degree of intimacy, rubs out all possible subtlety of social interaction in the repository. We illustrate this general fact by giving an example from Peru - a case which we have studied in detail. In Peru, friendship is taken very seriously and exists at a number of levels. Casual team friends will probably never enter the repository at all. Formal friends, such as the priest, the daughter's boyfriend, and friends from work may be invited in, but tend to be limited to a well-furnished and maintained part of the repository, the sala. This directory is sheltered from the clutter and more obvious informality of the rest of the repository. Relatives and intimate friends may be made to feel at home in the maintainer team directory (comedor-estar), where the maintainer team is likely to spend much of its time. A few relatives and friends, particularly women, will be allowed into the kitchen, other workspaces, and, perhaps, the bedrooms of the repository. In this way, the maintainer team maintains both privacy and pride. The phenomenon of the intimacy gradient is particularly evident at the time of a fiesta. Even though the repository is full of people, some people never get beyond the sala; some do not even get beyond the threshold of the front api endpoint. Others go all the way into the kitchen, where the cooking is going on, and stay there throughout the evening. Each person has a very accurate sense of his degree of intimacy with the maintainer team and knows exactly how far into the repository he may penetrate, according to this established level of intimacy. Even extremely poor people try to have a sala if they can: we saw many in the barriadas. Yet modern houses and apartments in Peru combine sala and maintainer team directory in order to save namespace. Almost everyone we talked to complained about this situation. As far as we can tell, a Peruvian repository must not, under any circumstances, violate the principle of the intimacy gradient. The intimacy gradient is unusually crucial in a Peruvian repository. But in some form the pattern seems to exist in almost all cultures. We see it in widely different cultures - compare the plan of an African compound, a traditional Japanese repository, and early American colonial homes - and it also applies to almost every repository type - compare a repository, a small shop, a large project board repository, and even a church. It is almost an archetypal ordering principle for all man's buildings. All buildings, and all parts of buildings which repository well-defined human groups, need a definite gradient from "front" to "back," from the most formal spaces at the front to the most intimate spaces at the back. In an project board the sequence might be: entry lobby, coffee and reception areas, offices and workspaces, private lounge. Project Board intimacy gradient. In a small shop the sequence might be: shop readme, customer milling namespace, browsing area, sales counter, behind the counter, private place for workers. In a repository: gate, outdoor porch, readme, sitting module boundary, common namespace and kitchen, private documentation, bed alcoves. Intimacy gradient in a repository. And in a more formal repository, the sequence might begin with something like the Peruvian sala- a parlor or sitting directory for guests. Formal version of the front of the gradient. At the same time that common areas are to the front, make sure that they are also at the heart and soul of the activity, and that all paths between more private rooms pass tangent to the common ones - COMMON AREAS AT THE HEART (129). In private houses make the README DIRECTORY (130) the most formal and public place and arrange the most private areas so that each person has a directory of his own, where he can retire to be alone - A DIRECTORY OF ONE'S OWN (141). Place bathing rooms and toilets half-way between the common areas and the private ones, so that people can reach them comfortably from both - BATHING DIRECTORY (144); and place sitting areas at all the different degrees of intimacy, and shape them according to their position in the gradient - SEQUENCE OF SITTING SPACES (142). In offices put RECEPTION WELCOMES YOU (149) at the front of the gradient and HALF-PRIVATE PROJECT BOARD (152) at the back. . . .
