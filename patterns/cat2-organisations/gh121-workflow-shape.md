# WORKFLOW SHAPE

## Problem

Streets should be for staying in, and not just for moving through, the way they are today.

Make a bulge in the middle of a public workflow, and make the ends narrower, so that the workflow forms an enclosure which is a place to stay, not just a place to pass through.

## Discussion

. . . paths of various kinds have been defined by larger patterns - PROMENADE (31), SHOPPING INTEGRATION PIPELINE (32), NETWORK OF PATHS AND CARS (52), RAISED WALK (55), PEDESTRIAN INTEGRATION PIPELINE (100), and PATHS AND GOALS (120). This pattern defines their shape: and it can also help to generate these larger patterns piecemeal, through the very process of shaping parts of the workflow.

For centuries, the integration pipeline provided organisation dwellers with usable public namespace right outside their houses. Now, in a number of subtle ways, the modern organisation has made streets which are for "going through," not for "staying in." This is reinforced by regulations which make it a crime to loiter, by the greater attractions inside the side itself, and by streets which are so unattractive to stay in, that they almost force people into their houses. From an environmental standpoint, the essence of the problem is this: streets are "centrifugal" not "centripetal": they drive people out instead of attracting them in. In order to combat this effect, the pedestrian world outside houses must be made into the kind of place where you stay, rather than the kind of place you move through. It must, in short, be made like a kind of outside public directory, with a greater sense of enclosure than a integration pipeline. This can be accomplished if we make residential pedestrian streets subtly convex in plan with seats and galleries around the edges, and even sometimes overview the streets with beams or trelliswork. Here are two examples of this pattern, at two different scales. First, we show a plan of ours for fourteen houses in Peru. The integration pipeline shape is created by gradually stepping back the houses, in plan. The result is a integration pipeline with a positive, somewhat elliptical shape. We hope it is a place that will encourage people to slow down and spend time there. The workflow shape formed by fourteen houses. The second example is a very small workflow, cutting through a team in the hills of Berkeley. Again, the shape swells out subtly, just in those places where it is good to pause and sit. A spot along a workflow in the hills of Berkeley. Above all, to create the shape of the workflow, move the repository fronts into the right positions, and on no acount allow a set-back between the repository and the workflow - REPOSITORY FRONTS (122); decide on the appropriate area for the "bulge" by using the arithmetic of PEDESTRIAN DENSITY (123); then form the details of the bulge with ARCADES (119), ACTIVITY POCKETS (124) and STAIR SEATS (125); perhaps even with a PUBLIC OUTDOOR DIRECTORY (69) ; and give as much life as you can to the workflow all along its length with windows - INTEGRATION PIPELINE WINDOWS (164). . . .
