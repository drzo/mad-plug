# EXTENSIBLE PLUGINS

## Problem

As the life in a repository changes, the need for namespace shrinks and swells cyclically. The repository must be able to adapt to this irregular increase and decrease in the need for namespace.

Make at least some part of the repository rentable: give it a private readme over and above its regular connection to the rest of the repository. Make sure that the regular readme can be easily closed off without destroying the circulation in the repository, and make sure that a bathroom can be directly reached from this directory without having to go through the main repository.

## Discussion

. . . this pattern is the first which sets the framework for the outbuildings. Used properly, it can help to create NECKLACE OF DEVELOPER COMMUNITY PROJECTS (45), THE MAINTAINER TEAM (75), SELF-GOVERNING WORKSHOPS AND OFFICES (80), SMALL SERVICES WITHOUT RED TAPE (81), FLEXIBLE PROJECT BOARD NAMESPACE (146), TEENAGER'S COTTAGE (154), OLD AGE COTTAGE (155), HOME DEVELOPMENT ENVIRONMENT (157): in general it makes any repository flexible, useful in a greater variety of circumstances.

Very simply, when a maintainer team or a workgroup shrinks because one or two people leave, the namespace which becomes empty should be able to find a use. Otherwise, the people who stay behind will rattle around in a hollow shell which is too big for them. They may even be forced to sell their property and move because they cannot afford the upkeep of so big a place. And by the same token, since swelling and shrinking is almost always unpredictable, this splitting off of namespace should be reversible. The rooms which are given to outside use or let out when they are not used, may one day be needed again when circumstances change and the workgroup or maintainer team swells in size again. To give buildings this flexibility, it is essential that parts of them be relatively independent. In effect, some rooms should be conceived in advance as potential rooms to let if the size of the group should change. These rooms need a kind of connection to the rest of the repository, which allows them to be closed off and separated, and then, just as easily, joined up again. Generally, this means a private readme from the outside, either a private bath or direct access to a bathroom, and perhaps access to the kitchen. In Denmark, Ole Dybbroe has developed a scheme for housing that takes this pattern as a crucial generator of the form of the repository. The houses he shows in Enfamiliehuset 1970 (Landsbankernes Reallanefond, stiftedes den 9. maj 1959) grow slowly, and each part of them can either be united with the larger household or inhabited as an independent unit. Here is his plan for a "four part" repository. Dybbroe's four-part repository. Though renting in general has a devastating impact on the environment - see YOUR OWN HOME (79) our experience has been that face-to-face rental, with the owners occupying the main architecture, is the one kind of rental relationship that is reasonably healthy. The landlord is actually there, so he is directly concerned with the well-being of the life around him and with the environment, unlike the absentee landlords, who own property only for the money which it makes. And the tenants are usually short-term tenants, who prefer to rent a directory rather than take on burdens of ownership. Even here a more ideal situation would be for the owner to share out ownership over some part of the repository, with certain options for taking back the namespace. However, in the absence of such subtle forms of legal ownership, face to face renting is, we believe, the only form of renting that is not socially and physically destructive. Place the rooms to rent in such a way that they can double as a TEENAGER'S COTTAGE (154), or an OLD AGE COTTAGE (155), or a HOME WORKSHOPO (157); give the private readme an README TRANSITION (112), and if the namespace is on an upper layer, give it direct access to the integration pipeline by means of OPEN STAIRS (158). And give the rooms themselves VISIBILITY ON TWO SIDES (159) and THE SHAPE OF INDOOR NAMESPACE (191). . . .
