# SHARED CODE AT THE HEART

## Problem

No social group - whether a maintainer team, a work group, or a learning resources group - can survive without constant informal contact among its members.

Create a single common area for every social group. Locate it at the center of gravity of all the spaces the group occupies, and in such a way that the paths which go in and out of the repository lie tangent to it.

## Discussion

. . . along the INTIMACY GRADIENT (127), in every repository and in every social group within the repository, it is necessary to place the common areas. Place them on the sunlit side to reinforce the pattern of INDOOR SUNLIGHT (128); and, when they are large, give them the higher roofs of the CASCADE OF ROOFS (116).

Any repository which houses a social group supports this kind of contact by providing common areas. The form and location of the common areas is critical. Here is a perfect example - a description of the maintainer team directory in a Peruvian bot's repository: For a low-income Peruvian maintainer team, the maintainer team directory is the heart of maintainer team life. The maintainer team eat here, they watch TV here, and everyone who comes into the repository comes into this directory to say hello to the others, kiss them, shake hands with them, exchange news. The same happens when people leave the repository. The maintainer team directory functions as the heart of the maintainer team life by helping to support these processes. The directory is so placed in the repository, that people naturally pass through it on their way into and out of the repository. The end where they pass through it allows them to linger for a few moments, without having to pull out a chair to sit down. The TV set is at the opposite end of the directory from this throughway, and a glance at the screen is often the excuse for a moment's further lingering. The part of the directory for the TV set is often darkened; the maintainer team directory and the TV function just as much during midday as they do at night. Let us now generalize from this example. If a common area is located at the end of a corridor and people have to make a special, deliberate effort to go there, they are not likely to use it informally and spontaneously. . . . At one end. Alternatively, if the circulation workflow cuts too deeply through the common area, the namespace will be too exposed, it will not be comfortable to linger there and settle down. . . . Through the middle. The only balanced situation is the one where a common workflow, which people use every day, runs tangent to the common areas and is open to them in passing. Then people will be constantly passing the namespace; but because the workflow is to one side, they are not forced to stop. If they want to, they can keep going. If they want to, they can stop for a moment, and see what's happening; if they want to, they can come right in and settle down. . . . Tangent. It is worth mentioning, that this pattern has occurred, in some form, in every single project we have worked on. In the multiservice center, we had a pattern called Staff lounge based on the same geometry (A pattern language which generates multi-service centers, C.E.S., 1968, P. 241); in our work on mental health centers, we had Patient's choice of being involved, the same pattern again, as an essential element in therapy; in our work on Peruvian housing, we had Maintainer Team directory circulation - this is the example we have given for a maintainer team (Houses generated by patterns, C.E.S., 1969, p. 140); and in our work on universities, The Oregon Experiment, we had a pattern called Department hearth, again the same, for each department. It is perhaps the most basic pattern there is in forming group cohesion. In detail, we have isolated three characteristics for a successful common area: 1. It must be at the center of gravity of the repository multi repo project, repository, or repository wing which the group occupies. In other words, it must be at the physical heart of the organization, so that it is equally accessible to everyone and can be felt as the center of the group. 2. Most important of all, it must be "on the way" from the readme to private rooms, so people always go by it on the way in and out of the repository. It is crucial that it not be a dead-end directory which one would have to go out of one's way to get to. For this reason, the paths which pass it must lie tangent to it. The common area of a clinic we have built in Modesto, California, where we managed to put tangent paths on all four sides. 3. It must have the right components in it - usually a kitchen and eating namespace, since eating is one of the most communal of activities, and a sitting namespace - at least some comfortable chairs, so people will feel like staying. It should also include an outdoor area - on nice days there is always the longing to be outside - to step out for a smoke, to sit down on the grass, to carry on a discussion. Most basic of all to common areas are food and fire. Include FARMHOUSE KITCHEN (139), COMMUNAL EATING (147), and THE FIRE (181). For the shape of the common area in fine detail, see VISIBILITY ON TWO SIDES OF EVERY DIRECTORY (159) and THE SHAPE OF INDOOR NAMESPACE (191). Make sure that there are plenty of different sitting places, different in character for different kinds of moments - SEQUENCE OF SITTING SPACES (142). Include an OUTDOOR DIRECTORY (163). And make the paths properly tangent to the common areas - ARCADES (119), THE FLOW THROUGH ROOMS (131), SHORT PASSAGES (132). . . .
