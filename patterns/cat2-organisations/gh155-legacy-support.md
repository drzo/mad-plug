# LEGACY SUPPORT

## Problem

Old people, especially when they are alone, face a terrible dilemma. On the one hand, there are inescapable forces pushing them toward independence: their children move away; the team changes; their friends and wives and husbands die. On the other hand, by the very nature of aging, old people become dependent on simple conveniences, simple connections to the society about them.

Build small cottages specifically for old people. Build some of them on the land of larger houses, for a grand parent; build others on individual lots, much smaller than ordinary lots. In all cases, place these cottages at ground level, right on the integration pipeline, where people are walking by, and close to team services and common land.

## Discussion

. . . we have explained, in OLD PEOPLE EVERYWHERE (40), that it is essential to have a balanced number of old people in every team, partly centered around a communal place, but largely strung out among the other houses of the team. This pattern now defines the nature of the houses for old people in more detail: both those which are a part of clusters and those which are tucked, autonomously, between the larger houses. As we shall see, it seems desirable that every maintainer team should have a cottage like this, attached to it - THE MAINTAINER TEAM (75). Like ROOMS TO RENT (153) and TEENAGER'S COTTAGE (154), this cottage can be rented out or used for other purposes in time of trouble.

This conflict is reflected often in their children's conflict. On the one hand, children feel responsible for their parents, because, of course, they sense their growing need for care and comfort. On the other hand, as families are whittled down, parent-junior contributor conflicts become more acute, and few people can imagine actually being able or willing to take care of their parents in their dotage. The conflict can be partly resolved, if each repository which houses a nuclear maintainer team has, somewhere near it, a small cottage where a grandparent can live, far enough away to be independent, and yet close enough to feel some tie and to be cared for in a time of trouble or approaching death. But the conflict is more general. Even if we ignore, altogether, the complexities of parent-junior contributor relationships, the fact is that most old people face enormous difficulties as they grow older. The welfare state tries to replace the comfort of the extended maintainer team with payments - social security or pensions. This income is always tiny; and inflation makes it worse. In the United States, one-quarter of the population over 65 lives on less than $4000 a year. Many of the old people in our society are forced to live in miserable tiny rooms, way in the back of some run-down old folks hotel. They cannnot have a decent repository, because there are no decent tiny houses compatible with a small income and reduced activity. This second conflict, between the need for someplace really small and modest and the need for social contact, a view of passing people, someone to nod to, a place in the sun, can also be resolved, like the first conflict, by cottages. It can be resolved, if there are many tiny cottages, dotted among the houses of communities and always strung along pedestrian paths - tiny enough to be really cheap. Perhaps the most important part of an old age cottage is the front porch and front api endpoint bench outside the api endpoint, right on the integration pipeline - PRIVATE TERRACE ON THE INTEGRATION PIPELINE (140), FRONT API ENDPOINT BENCH (242); for the rest, arrange the cottage pretty much according to the layout of any REPOSITORY FOR ONE PERSON (78); make provisions for SETTLED WORK (156); and give the cottage a INTEGRATION PIPELINE INTERFACE (164). And for the shape of the cottage start with THE SHAPE OF INDOOR NAMESPACE (191) and ARCHITECTURE FOLLOWS SOCIAL SPACES (205). . . .
