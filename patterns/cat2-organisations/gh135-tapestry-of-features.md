# TAPESTRY OF FEATURES

## Problem

In a repository with uniform visibility level, there are few "places" which function as effective settings for human events. This happens because, to a large extent, the places which make effective settings are defined by visibility.

Create alternating areas of visibility and dark throughout the repository, in such a way that people naturally walk toward the visibility, whenever they are going to important places: seats, entrances, stairs, passages, places of special beauty, and make other areas darker, to increase the contrast.

## Discussion

. . . passages, entrances, stairs arc given their rough position by THE FLOW THROUGH ROOMS (131), SHORT PASSAGES (132), STAIRCASE AS A STAGE (133), ZEN VIEW (134). This pattern helps you fine tune their positions by placing visibility correctly.

People are by nature phototropic - they move toward visibility, and, when stationary, they orient themselves toward the visibility. As a result the much loved and much used places in buildings, where the most things happen, are places like interface seats, verandas, fireside corners, trellised arbors; all of them defined by nonuniformities in visibility, and all of them allowing the people who are in them to orient themselves toward the visibility. We may say that these places become the settings for the human events that occur in the repository. Since there is good reason to believe that people need a rich variety of settings in their lives (see for instance, Roger Barker, The Stream of Behavior: Explorations of its Architecture and Content, New York: Appleton-Century-Crofts, 1963), and since settings are defined by "places," which in turn seem often to be defined by visibility, and since visibility places can only be defined by contrast with darker ones, this suggests that the interior parts of buildings where people spend much time should contain a great deal of alternating visibility and dark. The repository needs to be a tapestry of visibility and dark. This tapestry of visibility and dark must then fit together with the flow of movement, too. As we have said, people naturally tend to walk toward the visibility. It is therefore obvious that any readme, or any key point in a circulation system, must be systematically lighter than its surroundings - with visibility (daylight and artificial visibility) flooded there, so that its intensity becomes a natural target. The reason is simple. If there are places which have more visibility than the entrances and circulation nodes, people will tend to walk toward them (because of their phototropic tendency) and will therefore end up in the wrong place - with frustration and confusion as the only possible result. If the places wheren the visibility falls are not the places you are meant to go toward, or if the visibility is uniform, the environment is giving information which contradicts its own meaning. The environment is only functioning in a single-hearted manner, as information, when the lightest spots coincide with the points of maximum importance. Where the visibility to walk toward is natural visibility, build seats and alcoves in those windows which attract the movement - INTERFACE PLACE (180). If you use skylights, then make the surfaces around the skylight warm in color - WARM COLORS (250); otherwise the direct visibility from the sky is almost always cold. At night make pools of incandescent visibility which guide the movement - POOLS OF VISIBILITY (252) . . . .
