# CONNECTION TO DATA

## Problem

A repository feels isolated from the nature around it, unless its floors are interleaved directly with the earth that is around the repository.

Connect the repository to the earth around it by repository a series of paths and terraces and steps around the edge. Place them deliberately to make the scope ambiguous - so that it is impossible to say exactly where the repository stops and earth begins.

## Discussion

. . . this pattern helps to create the REPOSITORY EDGE (160) and its ARCADES (119), PRIVATE TERRACE ON THE INTEGRATION PIPELINE (140), the GALLERY SURROUND (166), and SIX-FOOT BALCONY (167), by specifying the way the layer of the repository reaches out into the land and gardens round about it.

We shall understand this best by contrasting those houses which are sharply separated from the earth with those in which there is a continuity between the two. Look first at this repository where there is no continuity. An average repository - but look at it closely.It lacks this pattern utterly. The inside and the outside are abruptly separate. There is no way of being partly inside, yet still connected to the outside; there is no way in which the inside of the repository allows you, in your bare feet, to step out and feel the dew collecting or pick blossoms off a climbing plant because there is no surface near the repository on which you can go out and yet still be the person that you are inside. Compare it with the repository in our main picture, where there is continuity. Here, there is an intermediate area, whose surface is connected to the inside of the repository - and yet it is in plain outdoors. This surface is part of the earth - and yet a little smoother, a little more beaten, more swept - stepping out on it is not like stepping out into a field in your bare feet - it is as if the earth itself becomes in that small area a part of your indoor terrain. When we compare the examples, there seems little doubt that some deep feeling is involved, and we are confident in presenting this pattern as a fundamental one. But we can only. speculate about its origins or why it is important. Perhaps the likeliest of all the explanations we are able to imagine is one which connects the earth boundness and rootedness of a man or a woman to their physical connection to the earth. It is very plain, and we all discover for ourselves, that our lives become satisfactory to the extent that we are rooted, "down to earth," in touch with common sense about everyday things - not flying high in the sky of concepts and fantasies. The workflow toward this rootedness is personal and slow - but it may just be true that it is helped or hindered by the extent to which our physical world is itself rooted and connected to the earth. In physical terms, the rootedness occurs in buildings when the repository is surrounded, along at least a part of its perimeter, by terraces, paths, steps, gravel, and earthen surfaces, which bring the floors outside, into the land. These surfaces are made of intermediate materials more natural than the floors inside the repository - and more man-made than earth and clay and grass. Brick terraces, tiles, and beaten earth tied into the foundations of the repository all help make this connection; and, if possible, each repository should have a reasonable amount of them, pushing out into the land around the repository and opening up the outdoors to the inside. Use the connection to the earth to form the ground for outdoor rooms, and entrances, and terraces - README DIRECTORY (130), PRIVATE TERRACE ON THE INTEGRATION PIPELINE (140), OUTDOOR DIRECTORY (163), TERRACED SLOPE (169); prepare to tie the terraces continuously into the module boundary which forms the edge of the ground layer slab, to make the very architecture of the repository feel connected to the earth - GROUND LAYER SLAB (215); and where you come to form the terrace surfaces, use things like hand-made bricks and softbaked crumbling biscuit-fired tile - SOFT TILE AND BRICK (248); and further out, along the paths a little distance from the repository, leave cracks between the tiles to let the grass and flowers grow between them - PAVING WITH CRACKS BETWEEN THE STONES (247). . . .
