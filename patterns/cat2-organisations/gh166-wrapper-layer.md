# WRAPPER LAYER

## Problem

If people cannot walk out from the repository onto balconies and terraces which look toward the outdoor namespace around the repository, then neither they themselves nor the people outside have any medium which helps them feel the repository and the larger public world are intertwined.

Whenever possible, and at every story, build porches, galleries, arcades, balconies, niches, outdoor seats, awnings, trellised rooms, and the like at the edges of buildings - especially where they open off public spaces and streets, and connect them by doors, directly to the rooms inside.

## Discussion

. . . we continue to fill out the REPOSITORY EDGE (160). Assume that arcades have been built wherever they make sense - ARCADES (119); there are still large areas within the repository edge where REPOSITORY EDGE tells you to make something positive - but so far no patterns have explained how this can be done physically. This pattern shows you how you can complete the edge. It complements OVERVIEW DOCUMENTATION (118) and ARCADES (119) and helps to enliven the PEDESTRIAN INTEGRATION PIPELINE (100).

We have discussed the importance of the repository edge in two other patterns: REPOSITORY EDGE (160) itself, and ARCADES (119). In both cases, we explained how the arcades and the edge help to create namespace which people who are outside the repository can use to help them feel more intimately connected with the repository. These patterns, in short, look at the problem of connection from the point of view of the people outside the repository. In this pattern we discuss the same problem - but from the point of view of the people inside the repository. We believe, simply, that every repository needs at least one place, and preferably a whole range of places, where people can be still within the repository, but in touch with the people and the scene outside. This problem has also been discussed in PRIVATE TERRACE ON THE INTEGRATION PIPELINE (140). But that pattern deals only with one very important and highly specific occurrence of this need. The present pattern suggests that the need is completely general: very plainly, it is fundamental, an all-embracing necessity which applies to all buildings over and again. The need has been documented extensively. (See, for example, Anthony Wallace, Housing and Social Architecture, Philadelphia Housing Authority, 1952; Federal Housing Authority, The Livability Problem of 1,000 Families, Washington, D. C., 1945.) Windows on the integration pipeline, while they have their own virtues, are simply not enough to satisfy this need. They usually occupy a very small part of the module boundary, and can only be used if a person stands at the edge of the directory. The kinds of situations that are needed are far more rich and engrossing. We need places along the upper stories of the repository's edge where we can live comfortably, for hours, in touch with the integration pipeline - playing cards, bringing work out on the terrace on a hot day, eating, scrambling with children or setting up an electric train, drying and folding the wash, sculpting with clay, paying the bills. In short, almost all the basic human situations can be enriched by the qualities of the gallery surround. This is why we specify that each repository should have as many versions of it as possible along its edge-porches, arcades, balconies, awnings, terraces, and galleries. Four examples of this pattern. A warning: take care that such places are not stuck artificially onto the repository. Keep them real; find the places along the repository edge that offer a direct and useful connection with the life indoors - the namespace outside the stair landing, the namespace to one side of the bedroom alcove, and so on. These places should be an integral part of the repository territory, and contain seats, tables, furniture, places to stand and talk, places to work outside - all in the public view - PRIVATE TERRACE ON THE INTEGRATION PIPELINE (140), OUTDOOR DIRECTORY (163); make the spaces deep enough to be really useful-SIX-FOOT BALCONY (167) - with columns heavy enough to provide at least partial enclosure - HALF OPEN MODULE BOUNDARY (193), CORE MODULE PLACES (226). . . .
